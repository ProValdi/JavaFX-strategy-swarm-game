Spatial Indexing in Games and Geospatial Applications

Alexander Korostin
16 min read
·
Jun 6, 2025

Motivation

Let’s start by defining what spatial indexing is and why it’s needed in application development. Spatial indexing reduces the computational cost of searching for a subset of spatially distributed objects within a specific area.

For example, when you open the Uber app on your phone, it doesn’t search through all the taxis registered in the system — it only looks for those located within a certain radius around your current position. Or take computer graphics: to keep the frame rate at an acceptable level, game engines or CAD software selectively render only the objects that fall within the camera’s field of view. This requires spatial querying. In all such cases, spatial indexing is typically used.

Why is this important? Without indexing, the complexity of finding the nearest points in a point cloud on a map is linear and depends on the total number of points. If the points are located on a sphere and specified by latitude and longitude, then for each point in the cloud, the distance to the center of the search circle needs to be calculated using the haversine formula.

unc Haversine(p1, p2 LngLat) float64 {
    dLat := (p2.Lat - p1.Lat) * degInRad
    dLon := (p2.Lng - p1.Lng) * degInRad
    lat1 := p1.Lat * degInRad
    lat2 := p2.Lat * degInRad
    sinDLat2 := math.Sin(dLat / 2)
    sinDLat2 *= sinDLat2
    sinDLon2 := math.Sin(dLon / 2)
    sinDLon2 *= sinDLon2
    a := sinDLat2 + math.Cos(lat1)*math.Cos(lat2)*sinDLon2
    return 2 * earthRadius * math.Atan2(math.Sqrt(a), math.Sqrt(1-a))
}

As you can see, this formula involves two square root calculations, two sine functions, two cosine functions, and one arctangent. Altogether, these make the function for computing the distance between two points on the surface of a spherical Earth model quite computationally heavy.

To demonstrate this, let’s run a benchmark:

func BenchmarkHaversine(b *testing.B) {
    p1 := LngLat{Lng: 1, Lat: 2}
    p2 := LngLat{Lng: 3, Lat: 4}
    for i := 0; i < b.N; i++ {
        Haversine(p1, p2)
    }
}

And we get the following results:

A speed of 103 nanoseconds per operation means that if your point cloud contains 1 million points, then forming a subset of points located within a certain circle will take at least 103 milliseconds — not including any additional overhead for memory allocation if needed. In other words, on a single CPU core, the application could perform at most 10 such queries per second.

Now, if you need to find the nearest neighbors for each point — for example, to implement Delaunay triangulation or DBSCAN clustering — then with an algorithmic complexity of O(n²), you’d be looking at 1.2 days of computation. And since the complexity is quadratic, clustering 10 million points would take about 4 months.

However, if you use spatial indexing, the cost of each subquery drops to O(log n), making it possible to cluster 10 million points in less than an hour, instead of 4 months.

In this article, we’ll explore the principles behind spatial indexing, examine popular data structures used to implement it, and evaluate their performance in practical scenarios. The goal is to understand how to choose and apply the right spatial index to significantly accelerate spatial queries in real-world applications.

QuadTree, Octree and HyperOctree

The QuadTree data structure is fairly simple both to understand and to implement. A two-dimensional space within certain bounds is divided into four equal rectangles or squares. Each of these squares is then recursively subdivided into four smaller nested squares, and this process continues until a specified maximum depth is reached. In total, this results in up to 4^N squares, where N is the maximum depth.

Each square holds spatial objects, typically defined by a bounding box. If a bounding box fits entirely within one of the child squares, it is passed down into that branch of the tree. In the case of a point cloud, points always “settle” in the leaf nodes this way.

However, it’s not necessary to create all levels of nesting. In fact, when spatial objects are distributed unevenly, it’s most efficient to strike a balance between the depth of the tree and the number of objects per leaf. For instance, traversing 10 parent bounding boxes just to find a single object would be a waste of resources. That’s why, in practice, along with the maximum depth, a minimum number of objects per leaf is usually defined as a threshold for further subdivision.

Since the number of search objects is divided by 4 at each level of nesting, and assuming a sufficiently uniform spatial distribution, the search complexity in a QuadTree is O(log n). Insertion, deletion, and moving objects in space have O(1) complexity. However, to achieve truly high performance, it’s important to apply additional optimizations that reduce the frequency of memory allocations — ideally, eliminating them altogether through techniques like buffering.

In graphics engines, branches of a QuadTree are often implemented as a special case of scene graph nodes for this very reason. The limitations of this data structure are clear: the space must be linear and two-dimensional.

The next data structure follows naturally from the previous one by simply adding a third spatial dimension: the Octree. Fundamentally, it works the same way, but within a 3D coordinate system. Octrees are primarily used in 3D game engines and CAD systems.

Theoretically, you can continue this pattern by adding any number of spatial dimensions. This structure is typically referred to as a HyperOctree whenever the number of dimensions exceeds three. However, in practice, HyperOctrees are rarely used, because increasing the number of dimensions exponentially increases the number of nested hypercubes. Instead, a far more practical alternative is the K-D Tree.

K-D tree

K-D Tree is a binary tree designed for organizing points in a space of arbitrary dimensionality K. Unlike QuadTrees or Octrees, which divide space into equal squares or cubes, a K-D Tree splits space based on the actual coordinate values of the points themselves.

Each node in the tree represents a point from the original dataset. When inserting a point, the algorithm selects one coordinate axis at each level of the tree (for example, X, then Y, then Z, and so on in a cycle), and compares values along that axis. Points with a smaller value go into the left subtree, while those with a greater or equal value go into the right subtree. In this way, the tree recursively divides space in half along one axis at a time, alternating axes at each level.

This method keeps the structure of the tree relatively balanced even in high-dimensional spaces, where traditional structures like QuadTrees become inefficient due to the exponential growth in the number of cells.

The complexity of nearest-neighbor search in a K-D Tree is the same as in a QuadTree — O(log n). However, unlike a QuadTree, the number of nodes in a K-D Tree is always equal to the number of points and does not depend on the number of dimensions. This makes the K-D Tree a default choice for indexing multidimensional points in many analytical libraries and machine learning frameworks.

Building a balanced tree takes O(n log n) time, and insertions, deletions, and point movements generally cost O(log n). As with other indexing structures, it’s recommended to avoid frequent rebuilding of the tree and to use buffering when inserting data in batches in order to achieve the best performance.

GeoHash

GeoHash is quite different from the tree-based spatial indexes we discussed earlier, like QuadTrees and K-D Trees. While those structures organize points by dividing space into nested regions or splitting by coordinate values, GeoHash takes a more straightforward approach: it turns geographic coordinates — latitude and longitude — into a simple, compact string.

Here’s how it works. Imagine the Earth’s surface as a big grid. GeoHash repeatedly divides this grid into smaller and smaller rectangles, encoding each subdivision into bits. These bits from latitude and longitude are then interleaved and converted into a short string using a special set of letters and numbers. The longer the string, the smaller the area it represents. For example, a GeoHash like “u4pruyd” might pinpoint a small neighborhood in a city, while just “u4pr” refers to a much larger region.

What makes GeoHash particularly handy is that points close to each other geographically often share the same starting characters in their hashes. So, if you want to find all locations near a certain point, you can quickly filter by matching the prefix of their GeoHashes. This is super useful when you’re working with databases or key-value stores because you can run fast prefix searches without complicated tree traversals.

To give a concrete example, say you have a delivery app and want to find all drivers within a few kilometers of a customer. Using GeoHash, you encode both driver and customer locations. Drivers whose GeoHashes start with the same few characters as the customer’s GeoHash are likely nearby, so you only need to check that smaller group rather than every driver in the system.

However, because GeoHash divides space into rectangles, it’s not perfect for exact circular or radius-based searches. After you narrow down candidates by GeoHash prefix, you usually need to do a more precise distance calculation to filter the results.

One limitation to keep in mind is that GeoHash works only with two dimensions — latitude and longitude. This is great for Earth-based locations but less suitable if you need to index points in higher-dimensional spaces like 3D models or time-series data.

R-tree

R-tree is another popular way to organize spatial data, but it works quite differently from both tree structures like K-D Trees and encoding methods like GeoHash. Think of R-tree as a way to group nearby objects together into nested rectangles, making it easier to find what’s close to what without checking every single point.

Here’s the basic idea: instead of storing individual points directly, R-tree stores bounding boxes — rectangles that contain one or more spatial objects. These rectangles are organized into a tree structure, where each node covers a certain area, and child nodes represent smaller rectangles inside that area. When you want to search for objects near a location, you start at the root and only explore those rectangles that might overlap with your search area, ignoring large portions of the space that definitely don’t contain relevant points.

For example, imagine a map application that shows parks, restaurants, and stores. Each park or restaurant is represented by a rectangle covering its location and size. The R-tree groups these rectangles into bigger rectangles — maybe grouping all parks in a neighborhood together, and then neighborhoods into districts, and so on. When a user searches for places nearby, the R-tree quickly narrows down which groups to check, making the search efficient even if there are thousands or millions of objects.

What makes R-tree powerful is that it can handle objects of various sizes and shapes because it works with bounding rectangles, not just points. This flexibility is a big advantage over K-D Trees or QuadTrees, which generally index points or uniformly sized cells.

In terms of performance, R-tree offers good average-case search times, usually around O(log n), where n is the number of objects. It also supports efficient insertion and deletion, making it practical for dynamic datasets where locations may change or be added frequently.

However, one challenge with R-trees is keeping the tree balanced and minimizing the overlap between bounding boxes. Too much overlap can slow down searches because more branches have to be explored. There are many variations of R-trees designed to address this, but the core idea remains the same.

BSP tree

Remember those classic 90s games like Doom and Quake? They relied on something called Binary Space Partitioning trees, or BSP trees for short. These were once the go-to solution for spatial data organization in computer graphics and game development, though they’ve mostly been replaced these days. Unlike other spatial structures that divide space into neat grids or boxes, BSP trees take a different approach — they slice up space using planes that can be oriented in any direction.

The process starts with your entire space (your game world), then creates a dividing plane through it. Everything on one side goes into the left branch and everything on the other side goes to the right branch. You continue making these divisions recursively, creating smaller and smaller regions. The real advantage was that you could position these dividing planes anywhere that made sense for your particular scene — no need to follow a rigid grid pattern.

BSP trees had their heyday with those groundbreaking games of the 90s, helping create 3D worlds on computers that were incredibly limited by today’s standards! When a player looked around in Doom, the game used its BSP tree to quickly determine which walls were visible and which weren’t, without wasting precious computing power checking everything in the level.

These versatile trees could represent complex environments with impressive precision. But — and here’s why they’ve largely been replaced — they came with some significant downsides. Building a BSP tree becomes increasingly complex as your world gets bigger. And if you wanted to move objects around dynamically (which is essential in modern games), updating the tree becomes extremely difficult.

Back in the day, BSP trees were crucial because they helped games run smoothly when processing power was at a premium. They efficiently eliminated parts of the scene that players couldn’t see, making real-time gaming possible on the hardware available then.

H3 by Uber

H3 is one of today’s leading geospatial indexing systems, developed by Uber in 2018 and now widely adopted throughout the industry. Unlike the outdated BSP trees we discussed earlier, H3 was designed specifically for the demands of modern location-based services and big data applications.

H3 divides the Earth’s surface into hexagonal cells at multiple resolutions. This hexagonal grid system starts with 122 base cells that cover the planet, and each cell can be further subdivided into seven smaller hexagons, creating a hierarchical structure with increasing precision at each level. At its finest resolution (level 15), H3 cells measure just a few centimeters across.

The system gained rapid popularity after Uber open-sourced it, and for good reason. The hexagonal approach offers significant advantages over traditional square grids. Hexagons have uniform adjacency — each cell has exactly six neighbors at the same distance — which makes calculating movements and proximity much more accurate and consistent. This property is particularly valuable for ride-sharing, delivery services, and similar applications where distance calculations directly affect user experience.

H3 addresses many limitations that plagued older spatial indexing systems. Its hierarchical nature makes it extremely efficient for operations like spatial joins and aggregations. When you need to analyze data at different scales — say, looking at ride demand patterns at neighborhood level versus city level — H3 makes this process remarkably straightforward. The uniform cell sizes at each resolution also eliminate the distortion problems that challenge many other global grid systems.

The H3 ecosystem now includes implementations in numerous programming languages, visualization tools, and integration with popular geospatial libraries.

Google S2

S2 represents another cutting-edge approach to geospatial indexing that’s widely deployed in production systems today. Developed by Google around 2011 and later open-sourced, S2 takes a fundamentally different approach from H3 while solving many of the same core problems in spatial indexing.

Unlike H3’s hexagonal tiling, S2 employs a hierarchical quadtree structure based on a cube projection of the sphere. The implementation maps the Earth onto six faces of a cube, which are then recursively subdivided into four cells each, creating a multi-resolution grid. These cells are addressed using Hilbert curve indexing, which preserves locality exceptionally well — points that are close in physical space tend to have similar cell IDs.

S2 excels in computational geometry operations that many other geospatial systems struggle with. It’s not just an indexing system but a geometry library that handles complex polygon operations, coverings, and spatial relationships. The library includes algorithms for point-in-polygon tests, polygon unions, intersections, and differences — all operations that are notoriously difficult to implement correctly on a sphere.

Performance is where S2 truly shines. Google Maps, Google Earth, and numerous other Google services rely on S2 for their geospatial operations, processing billions of queries daily.

A key technical advantage of S2 is its handling of projection distortion. By using a cube projection rather than a single flat projection, S2 minimizes distortion issues that plague many other global indexing systems, particularly near the poles. This makes S2 especially well-suited for applications requiring consistent spatial accuracy worldwide.

S2 definitely takes more time to master than other options out there, but its powerful features make it the go-to choice when you need to handle complex spatial operations.

BHV

Bounding Volume Hierarchy (BVH) is a spatial data structure widely used in modern computer graphics, particularly in real-time rendering, ray tracing, and physics simulations. It reflects contemporary demands — handling complex, dynamic 3D environments with high efficiency — unlike older methods such as the BSP tree, which was developed at a time when static geometry and software rasterization were the norm.

The principle behind BVH is straightforward: instead of dividing space itself, it organizes objects by grouping nearby ones into bounding volumes — typically axis-aligned bounding boxes (AABBs) — and builds a binary tree where each node encapsulates the bounds of its children. When querying the tree (for example, casting a ray or checking for collisions), large parts of the scene can be skipped entirely if the bounding box at a node doesn’t intersect the query. This results in significant performance gains, particularly in applications where geometric tests are expensive.

One of the strengths of BVH is its adaptability. Unlike Octrees, which divide space evenly into eight cubes regardless of the distribution or size of the objects, BVH structures conform to the actual arrangement of the scene. Octrees can be very effective when the data is uniformly distributed — such as voxel-based representations or volumetric simulations — but in scenes with sparse, clustered, or dynamic objects, they tend to waste memory and processing power subdividing empty regions. Furthermore, handling moving objects in an Octree is cumbersome, as any change in position may require the object to be reassigned within the tree structure.

BVH handles dynamic content much more gracefully. Since the structure is object-centric, it can be rebuilt or incrementally updated with minimal overhead, even in real time. Techniques like bounding box refitting and surface area heuristics allow BVH trees to adapt efficiently as the scene evolves — something that’s increasingly important in modern game engines and real-time ray tracers.

Comparing BVH to R-tree, the difference lies in the intended use case and traversal efficiency. R-trees also use bounding volumes to group spatial objects, but they are optimized for database-style queries in GIS systems and spatial indexing in 2D or 3D space. R-trees are multi-way trees and are designed to stay balanced across insertions and deletions, prioritizing general-purpose spatial querying over traversal speed. However, because bounding volumes in R-trees often overlap significantly, traversing them may require checking multiple branches, which can lead to performance bottlenecks in real-time applications.

BVH trees, by contrast, are usually binary and constructed to minimize overlap and surface area, leading to shallower trees and faster traversal. This makes them particularly well-suited for ray-geometry intersection tests, where each unnecessary bounding box traversal represents wasted computation.

Hash Grids

Hash Grids are a simple yet powerful spatial indexing technique often used in real-time applications such as particle systems, fluid simulations, and proximity queries. Unlike tree-based structures like BVH, Octree, or R-tree, which rely on hierarchical subdivision, Hash Grids flatten space into a uniform grid and use hashing to store and retrieve spatial data efficiently. This makes them extremely fast for certain tasks, particularly when performance and simplicity are more important than exact spatial organization.

The concept is easy to grasp. The space is divided into a regular 2D or 3D grid of cells. Each object is mapped to a cell based on its position — essentially by dividing its coordinates by the grid resolution and rounding to the nearest integer. Instead of storing all these cells in memory (which would be impractical for sparse or infinite spaces), the cell coordinates are passed through a hash function, which maps them to a slot in a hash table. Objects that fall into the same cell (or hash slot) are stored together in a list.

When a query is made — like finding all objects near a certain point — you simply compute which cell the point belongs to and check that cell and its neighbors. Since objects are grouped locally, most queries require only a few lookups and comparisons. The time complexity for insertion and lookup is O(1) on average, which is a major advantage in performance-critical scenarios.

Compared to BVH, Hash Grids are much simpler and more lightweight. There’s no hierarchy, no recursive splitting, and no bounding boxes to maintain. This makes them ideal for dynamic environments where objects are constantly being added, removed, or moved — such as in particle-based simulations or real-time crowd systems. However, this simplicity comes with trade-offs.

Unlike BVH or R-tree, Hash Grids are not well suited for scenes with a wide variation in object sizes or densities. The fixed grid resolution means that very large objects may span many cells, and very small objects may all cluster in the same cell, which can degrade performance. Also, because hash functions can create collisions (multiple cells mapping to the same slot), careful design and tuning are required to avoid performance pitfalls in large datasets.

Compared to Octrees, which subdivide space recursively and adapt to scene depth, Hash Grids operate on a flat structure. They don’t adapt to the data — they expect the developer to choose a good cell size ahead of time. In uniform scenes (like liquid simulations or evenly distributed particles), this works beautifully. In uneven or clustered data, a poorly chosen resolution can result in too many empty cells or overloaded buckets.

Even with their limitations, Hash Grids are still a favorite in many real-time systems — mainly because they’re fast and easy to implement. When you’re working with data that’s fairly evenly spread out and constantly changing, and you can live with a fixed resolution, Hash Grids often hit the sweet spot between speed and simplicity. In fact, in the right context, they can even outperform more sophisticated structures like BVH or R-tree. It’s a good reminder that sometimes, the simplest approach really is the most effective.
